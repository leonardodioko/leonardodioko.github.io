Going against the crowd

The most enduring principle in investment history may also be the most psychologically uncomfortable: the greatest returns flow not toward the assets everyone is buying, but toward those everyone is ignoring.

The most enduring principle in investment history may also be the most psychologically uncomfortable: the greatest returns flow not toward the assets everyone is buying, but toward those everyone is ignoring. Contrarian investing — the discipline of deploying capital where consensus attention is absent — is not mere perversity. It is a rational response to how markets actually misprice assets.

Markets are not merely mechanisms for price discovery; they are machines for aggregating human emotion. When a sector captivates the imagination of investors, capital floods in, valuations inflate, and the margin of safety evaporates. The late 1990s dotcom boom is the canonical case. Between 1995 and 2000, the NASDAQ Composite rose roughly 400%. Investors poured money into any company bearing a ".com" suffix, regardless of earnings, cash flow, or business model. Meanwhile, deeply unglamorous sectors — energy, financials, and old-economy industrials — were systematically shunned. A contrarian who loaded up on ExxonMobil, Berkshire Hathaway, or Procter & Gamble in 1999, while the crowd chased Pets.com and Webvan, would have dramatically outperformed over the following decade.

The same dynamic played out before the global financial crisis of 2008. As housing-related assets and financial stocks consumed investor imagination — banks, mortgage originators, and structured credit vehicles generated enormous enthusiasm — the overlooked opportunities sat in plain sight. Commodities and emerging markets were deeply unfashionable in the early 2000s yet subsequently delivered extraordinary returns. Jim Rogers, Marc Faber, and other contrarians who identified agricultural commodities and frontier markets as undervalued well before mainstream recognition captured multi-year bull markets that consensus investors missed entirely.

The academic evidence supports this thesis. The value premium — documented extensively by Fama and French — demonstrates that cheap, unloved stocks have historically outperformed expensive, beloved ones over long horizons. Behavioral finance research by Kahneman, Thaler, and others shows that investors systematically extrapolate recent trends, overpaying for recent winners and abandoning recent losers. This creates the structural mispricings that contrarian investors exploit. Research by Lakonishok, Shleifer, and Vishny found that value stocks (the overlooked) outperformed glamour stocks (the beloved) by approximately 10–11 percentage points annually over five-year holding periods in U.S. markets.

The contrarian thesis does not require predicting the future. It requires only recognizing when the price of an asset reflects excessive pessimism — when the margin of safety is generous precisely because the crowd has fled. Warren Buffett's maxim — "be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful" — is not a platitude. It is a compression of the entire contrarian argument into a single sentence. His purchase of American Express during the 1963 salad oil scandal, or his buying of Washington Post shares in 1973 when the media sector was reviled, generated some of the most celebrated returns in investment history.

The difficulty, of course, is psychological. Being contrarian means being wrong in the company of the crowd for uncomfortably long periods before being right alone. But that discomfort is precisely the source of the return. If superior investment opportunities were obvious and comfortable, they would already be priced away. The alpha available to the contrarian investor is, at its core, a premium for enduring unpopularity.The charts illustrate the thesis starkly across both historical episodes. In the dotcom era, the consensus bet on NASDAQ technology names produced a spectacular rise and catastrophic collapse, while S&P 500 Energy — completely ignored during the mania — compounded steadily throughout. The pre-GFC pattern is nearly identical: US financials peaked and then cratered, while commodities and emerging markets (the contrarian trade of the early 2000s) delivered dramatically superior cumulative returns even accounting for their own 2008 drawdown.

The lesson across both periods is consistent: the crowd's enthusiasm is precisely what destroys the return. By the time a sector is widely loved, its upside is already priced in — and its downside is not.
